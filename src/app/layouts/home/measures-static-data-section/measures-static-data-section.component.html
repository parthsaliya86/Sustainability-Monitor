What are Sustainability Reports?

Sustainability reports are documents in which firms disclose information about their environmental and social impact, as well as on aspects related to their corporate governance. The most common format is PDF files, but sustainability reports may also exist in form of tables or presentation slides. They are typically published once a year or every two years.

Not all firms publish sustainability reports. Some prefer to integrate this information in a specific section of their annual financial reports, while others disclose little to no information, as sustainability reporting is still voluntary for many firms.

Firms can use different reporting frameworks or a combination of them, such as the framework from the Global Reporting Initiative (GRI), the Accounting Standards Board (SASB), the Task Force on Climate-Related Financial Disclosures (TCFD), or the Nations Principles for Responsible Investment (PRI) for the financial services sector. Therefore, comparing the disclosures across different firms and sectors is still very challenging.

Environmental, Social, and Governance Indicators

Environmental In the environmental area, firms may communicate to their stakeholders their current greenhouse gas emissions, water, and energy consumption levels, as well as their medium and long-term goals to reduce their environmental footprint. When measuring their greenhouse gas emissions, firms may consider three different dimensions:

  • Scope 1 emissions: Emissions produced by the firms’ own facilities and activities, for example, their factories or vehicles.
  • Scope 2 emissions: Emissions related to generating the energy the firms consume to execute their activities.
  • Scope 3 emissions: Emissions related to upstream or downstream activities by other parties in the value chain, for example, firms’ suppliers and distributors, or how the sold products are used. Most firms report on scope 1 emissions but reporting on scope 2 and 3 emissions are progressively more challenging, due to the lack of available data and methodological difficulties in attributing emissions along the value chain. Social

When discussing their social impact, firms may report on several indicators and activities that aim at creating a positive impact on their employees, customers, supply chains, and the communities where they act. These may include indicators and qualitative data about their workforce diversity, employee turnover, or on their investments to develop local communities. Governance

In the governance area, firms can report on a broad range of internal and external activities, such as their board composition, compliance programs, donations, political contributions, or their share of business in locations with a high corruption index. This portion of the reports typically contains more qualitative data and less indicators that allow stakeholders to quantify firms’ impact and risks.

Sustainability, Reporting, Directives in the European Union

In the European Union (EU), since the passing of the Non-Financial Reporting Directive (NFRD) in 2014, large public-interest firms with more than 500 employees need to report on certain ESG indicators since 2017. This directive was followed by additional guidelines for the disclosure of environmental and social information in 2017, and complemented with the guidelines for climate-related reporting in 2019 and applied to approximately 11,000 firms.

However, sustainability reports are mostly still voluntary and hard to compare, as there are many (non-binding) standards available. Therefore, most firms may currently decide whether, what and how to report on their ESG impact. The reported information is rarely validated by an independent third-party.

In an effort to expand the scope and increase the comparability of sustainability reports, the European Commission recently adopted a package of measures towards a binding classification system, or taxonomy. These include the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), passed in 2021. This directive applies to all publicly listed firms, including small and medium enterprises (SME), which amount to approximately 50.000 firms. Non-European firms with at least one subsidiary or branch in the EU and a local net turnover of €150 million are also subject to this directive.

The CSRD establishes mandatory ESG reporting standards and requires that the reported information is audited, which is an important step towards the reliability and accuracy of the ESG indicators. It applies in January 2024 (reporting year 2023) for firms already subject to the NFRD, extends to large firms not currently covered by the NFRD in 2025 and to publicly listed SMEs in 2026.

The CSRD is complemented by the Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation (SFDR), which applies to the financial services sector and is binding in January 2023. The German Federal Financial Supervisory Authority (BaFin) is also working on a Guideline for Sustainable Funds, which is expected to provide directives for ESG investment funds. The public consultation started in 2021 but the ensuing works have been recently put on hold.

Other global efforts to simplify ESG reporting include the consolidation of the International Integrated Reporting Council (IIRC) and the SASB into the Value Reporting Foundation (VRF) in 2021. The VRF was further consolidated with the Carbon Disclosure Standards Board (CDSB) to establish the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) in 2022. Its proposed standards also build upon recommendations from the TCFD.
